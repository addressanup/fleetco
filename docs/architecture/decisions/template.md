## Context

What is the problem being solved? What constraints are in play? What is the situation that requires this decision to be made? Write enough that a future reader who has no context from this moment can understand why the question was open. The reader you are writing for is a fresh agent or a fresh you, six months from now, with no memory of this session.

## Decision

What did we decide? Be specific. State the decision in a single sentence at the start, then expand with as much detail as the decision needs. The decision is the most important part of the document; subsequent sections explain why.

## Alternatives considered

What else did we look at, and why did we reject each one? Each alternative gets a brief explanation of what it would have meant and why it lost. This section is the one that protects the project from re-litigating the decision in future sessions, because it is the section that anticipates and answers the question "but did you consider X?" before the question is asked.

## Consequences

What does this decision make easier? What does it make harder? What costs are we accepting? Be honest, especially about the costs. A decision with no acknowledged costs is a decision that has not been thought through.

## Revisit when

What signal would tell us that this decision should be reopened? Specific, observable signals are better than vague ones. "When we have a thousand users" is observable; "when scale becomes a problem" is not. If no clear signal exists, write that the decision is unlikely to be revisited and explain why.
